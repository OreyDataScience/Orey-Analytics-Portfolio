# Insight Summary – SME Cashflow Analysis

## Overview

This analysis evaluates the cashflow performance of an SME over a 3-month period (January–March).

## Key Findings

### Cashflow Trends

* January: -448,120
* February: -302,172
* March: +385,557

The business experienced two consecutive months of negative cashflow before recovering in March. This indicates early liquidity pressure, possible seasonality or delayed revenue inflows.

### Profitability

* Total Income: 9,878,428
* Total Expenses: -10,243,163
* Net Loss: -364,735

The business is operating at a loss despite strong revenue generation. Despite a strong March, it did not fully recover prior losses. Could be a temporary recovery masking underlying structural inefficiency.


### Cost Drivers

* Highest expense categories:

  * Consulting
  * Utilities
  * Software
  * Travel

* These categories contribute significantly to overall financial pressure. 
* Having more than one cost indicates a broad operational cost base and potential lack of cost control across multiple areas. 
* Core operational categories are destroying value and the business is spending heavily in areas that are not generating proportional returns.

### Revenue Insights

* Revenue is diversified across categories
* However, some high-revenue categories are still unprofitable
* Marketing is the only category that is meaningfully profitable. 

### Geographic Insights

* Cape Town generates the highest revenue but also has the highest costs
* Indicates a major operational hub with potential inefficiencies

### Key Risk Indicators

1. Sustained negative cashflow (2 out of 3 months are negative)
2. Structural loss (Overall net loss despite strong revenue)
3. Inefficient cost allocation (High spending in low-return categories)
4. Geographic cost pressure (Cape Town heavily impacting profitability)

### Strategic Interpretation

* Weak cost management
* Poor ROI tracking per category
* Possible over-investment in operations

## Conclusion

The business demonstrates strong revenue generation but lacks cost efficiency, resulting in an overall loss. Improved cost management and better allocation of resources are required to achieve profitability.

Without intervention:
1. Continued losses are likely
2. Cashflow instability may persist
